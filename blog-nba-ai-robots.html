How Players Train

Physical AI Comes to the NBA

At the 2025 NBA All-Star Technology Summit in San Francisco — marking 25 years since the league's first Tech Summit — NBA Commissioner Adam Silver and the Golden State Warriors unveiled a suite of Physical AI robots built specifically for basketball training. The announcement was not a concept or a prototype showcase. The Warriors were already using the robots in practice sessions before the summit took place.

The initiative represents the most significant shift in on-court skills training methodology since wearable GPS trackers became standard in the early 2010s. Where previous technology tracked what players did, these robots actively participate in the training session alongside them.

The Four Robots and What They Do

A.B.E. — Automated Basketball Engine is the most visible of the four. It functions as a tireless shooting partner: chasing every rebound, calculating the ball's trajectory the instant it leaves a player's hands, and delivering pinpoint return passes to the shooter's preferred spot. It eliminates all dead time between repetitions, which is where large volumes of high-quality shooting practice are typically lost.

M.I.M.I.C. — Motion and Intercept Modular-Interface Coordination acts as a defensive simulator. Coaches programme it to replicate specific opponents' defensive tendencies. It does not fatigue, does not fall for the same move twice, and reacts in real time to a player's positioning and movement.

M.I.M.I.C. provides defenders and offensive players alike with game-realistic pressure during drills — something no cone or stationary defender can replicate.

K.I.T. — Kinematic Interface Tool addresses the mental side of performance. It interacts conversationally with players, delivers motivational support during demanding workouts, and replicates the psychological support role a sports psychologist or assistant coach might play during long training blocks.

B.E.B.E. — Bot Enhanced Basics and Equipment focuses on physical readiness and recovery. It monitors pre-session preparation and post-game recovery protocols — recommending stretching routines, ice bath timing, and hydration interventions based on real-time physical data gathered during training.

Stephen Curry and Steve Kerr Weigh In

Stephen Curry, whose shooting practice volume is among the highest in the league, described his first sessions with A.B.E. as initially unusual — then quickly came around. With no need to retrieve balls or wait for a rebounder to reset, Curry's effective shooting repetitions per session increased substantially.

Head coach Steve Kerr has integrated the robots into practice design as if they were members of the training staff. He directs M.I.M.I.C. with verbal commands during drills, repositioning it mid-play to simulate different defensive coverages — issuing the same instructions to the robots that he would give to a human practice squad player.

What This Means for Player Development

The core training principle behind A.B.E. is straightforward: more quality repetitions in less time. Traditional shooting sessions are constrained by the logistics of ball retrieval and rebounder fatigue. A.B.E. removes both constraints entirely. A player who previously completed 300 high-quality shot attempts in an hour can now exceed that volume with better reset tempo and no interruption to rhythm.

M.I.M.I.C. addresses a longstanding problem in individual skills training: the absence of live defensive resistance. Most solo workouts involve stationary defenders or cones, which do not replicate game pressure. A defender that moves, reacts, and adjusts in real time changes the difficulty and the transfer of a drill to actual competition.

The B.E.B.E. recovery system speaks to a growing understanding in sports science that adaptation happens between training sessions, not during them. Monitoring recovery quality and automating personalised recovery protocols reduces the injury risk that comes with high-repetition training blocks.

Taken together, the four robots address the four factors that most constrain individual skills development: repetition volume, defensive realism, mental sustainability, and recovery quality.

Is This Coming to Every Gym?

As of early 2026, the technology remains in the hands of a small number of well-resourced NBA programs. Cost, maintenance requirements, and the need for technical staff to calibrate the systems all limit near-term adoption. The NBA has indicated it expects broader league-wide rollout as the technology matures and costs decrease.

Parallel developments in the broader market are moving in the same direction. AI-powered video analysis platforms now offer shooting form analysis, heat mapping, and drill personalisation to players at any level. Systems like HomeCourt and DribbleUp have made AI-assisted feedback accessible to youth players and club programs. The gap between NBA-level training infrastructure and what is available to serious developing players is narrowing quickly.

Research published in Scientific Reports in January 2026 outlined a randomised trial testing an AI-driven personalised training system for youth basketball players aged 12–18, designed to dynamically adjust training loads based on real-time performance data — signalling the direction the entire sport is heading.

What Players Should Take From This

The introduction of Physical AI into NBA training carries a direct message for developing players: the training environment is changing, and so are the standards it produces. Players who use every available repetition maximally — treating each drill as if a robot were tracking their mechanics — will adapt faster than those who train with low intentionality.

The robots do not replace good coaching or structured programs. They amplify what is already in place. A poorly designed session run with a robot is still a poorly designed session. The quality of the training framework matters first. Volume and feedback technology sit on top of that foundation, not in place of it.

For players targeting college recruitment and high-level competition, the takeaway is practical: track your repetitions, seek feedback on mechanics from qualified coaches, and understand that the athletes you are competing against now have access to tools that optimise every minute of their development time. The standard is rising. Training has to rise with it.
