The ACL Return-to-Play Problem

ACL reconstruction is one of the most common surgeries in basketball. The injury rate in collegiate and semi-professional basketball sits at 0.16 per 1,000 athlete-exposures, and in high school players it is 0.06 per 1,000 — figures that translate to hundreds of surgeries every season across Australia and the United States. Approximately one in three young athletes who return to sport following ACL reconstruction will suffer a second ACL injury. That reinjury rate has not moved meaningfully in decades.

The core problem is that return-to-play decisions have historically been made without adequate objective testing. Time since surgery, self-reported readiness, and basic single-leg hop tests have been the dominant tools. What has been missing is a reliable way to detect the subtle movement asymmetries that persist in a surgically repaired knee — asymmetries that often remain invisible to standard clinical assessments but represent real re-injury risk when a player returns to the explosive demands of the sport.

Force plate technology is changing that. And in July 2025, the most targeted research yet on this problem — specific to basketball athletes, specific to force plate metrics, and specific to the final stages of post-operative rehabilitation — was published in the International Journal of Sports Physical Therapy.

The Study: Detroit Pistons Physio Co-Authors Landmark Research

The study was co-authored by Christopher S. Hart, the physical therapist in the Medical and Performance Department of the NBA's Detroit Pistons, alongside Elizabeth S. Chumanov, PhD, from the Sports Rehabilitation department at UW Health University Hospital. Its publication in a peer-reviewed journal marks the first time the specific combination of force plate jump metrics most relevant to basketball ACL return-to-play decisions has been formally investigated and reported for this population.

The focus was precise: not whether force plates are useful in ACL rehabilitation generally — that is already established — but which specific jump tasks and which specific metrics within those tasks best detect the asymmetries that matter for basketball-specific return-to-play clearance.

What They Tested and How

Forty-nine male high school and collegiate basketball athletes who had undergone primary ACL reconstruction were assessed using a dual force plate system during the final stages of rehabilitation. All participants had undergone either patellar tendon autograft or hamstring tendon autograft procedures. The study used a retrospective cohort design, drawing on the UW Health database.

Participants were split into two groups based on isokinetic quadriceps peak torque limb symmetry index (LSI): those who had achieved above 90% LSI (indicating stronger quad symmetry between limbs) and those who remained below 90% LSI. The 90% threshold is the established benchmark in ACL rehabilitation literature as the minimum acceptable level of quadriceps strength symmetry for return-to-play clearance.

Three jumping tasks were assessed on the force plates: the double-leg countermovement jump (DL-CMJ), the single-leg countermovement jump (SL-CMJ), and the single-leg repeat hop (SL-RH). Dozens of metrics were extracted from the force-time curves of each jump — including eccentric rate of force development, concentric impulse, braking force, peak take-off force, flight time, contact time, and reactive strength index (flight time to contact time ratio).

What the Force Plates Revealed

The results were specific and clinically significant. Statistically significant differences in limb symmetry index between the two groups (above and below 90% quad strength) were found on two of the three jump tasks — the DL-CMJ and the SL-RH — but not on the SL-CMJ.

In the double-leg countermovement jump, five metrics showed significant differences between groups. Eccentric rate of force development showed a mean difference of -22.6 between groups. Concentric impulse showed a mean difference of -22.4. Concentric impulse in the first 100 milliseconds showed a mean difference of -24.0. Contact time showed a mean difference of -0.43 seconds. Peak take-off force showed a mean difference of -12.7. All differences were significant at p < 0.05.

In the single-leg repeat hop, two metrics were significant: jump height (mean difference -12.4) and flight time to contact time ratio (mean difference 13.3). These are metrics that capture reactive strength — the ability to rapidly absorb and redirect force between landings — which is a physical quality central to basketball's repeated jumping demands.

The Metric That Matters Most: Concentric Impulse

Concentric impulse — the area under the force-time curve during the propulsive phase of a jump — emerged as the single most consistent metric across the study. It was the only variable that showed statistically significant between-limb differences across all three jump tasks tested, making it the strongest candidate as a primary force plate metric for basketball ACL return-to-play assessment.

Concentric impulse measures how much total force a limb produces during the push-off phase. In a player with residual quad weakness or neuromuscular inhibition in the reconstructed limb, the involved leg produces measurably less impulse than the uninvolved leg — a difference that is invisible to the naked eye, typically does not show up in basic hop tests, but is captured precisely by a dual force plate system sampling at high frequency.

The clinical implication is direct. A basketball player who clears a 90% limb symmetry index on a hop test can still carry a 20%+ deficit in concentric impulse that will affect their ability to safely generate and absorb force during in-game jumps. That deficit, if undetected, is a return-to-play risk.

The Single-Leg Jump Surprise

One of the study's most practically important findings was the absence of significant between-group differences during the single-leg countermovement jump. This runs counter to common clinical practice, where the SL-CMJ is often used as a primary return-to-play test on the assumption that it is more demanding of the reconstructed limb than bilateral tests.

The authors suggest the SL-CMJ may not be sensitive enough to detect the asymmetries that persist in the final stages of ACLR rehabilitation — particularly in athletes who have become skilled at compensating on a single-leg task through altered technique. By contrast, the reactive double-leg tasks and the single-leg repeat hop — which remove the athlete's ability to control loading through self-selected countermovement depth — revealed the asymmetries the SL-CMJ masked.

This finding argues for a battery of jump tests, not a single test. The study's conclusion states explicitly that no single jump task captures the full picture of physical readiness for return to basketball after ACL reconstruction.

What This Means for Basketball Rehab

The practical implications for physiotherapists, strength and conditioning coaches, and athletic performance staff working with basketball players post-ACLR are clear. First, force plate testing should be integrated into the final stages of ACL rehabilitation — not just as a standalone assessment but as a monitoring tool that tracks asymmetry trends across multiple sessions as the athlete progresses toward clearance.

Second, a minimum battery of three jump tasks is warranted: a bilateral reactive task (DL-CMJ), a unilateral reactive task (SL-RH), and the SL-CMJ. The bilateral and reactive tasks appear more sensitive to the underlying asymmetries that standard clinical testing misses. Clearing on the SL-CMJ alone is not sufficient.

Third, concentric impulse should be the primary metric tracked, given its consistent sensitivity across tasks. Eccentric rate of force development and reactive strength index (flight time:contact time) are secondary metrics that add meaningful information about different components of jump performance.

Fourth, the 90% quad LSI threshold remains the standard gate for return-to-play clearance — but force plate testing provides a parallel check on whether that strength symmetry is translating into movement symmetry under dynamic, reactive conditions. A player can achieve 90% quad LSI while still showing a 20%+ concentric impulse deficit. Both gates need to be cleared, not just one.

The Wider Research Picture

This study sits within a rapidly growing body of 2025 research validating force plate technology across the ACL rehabilitation continuum. A September 2025 systematic review published in Cureus surveyed biomechanical assessment tools for injury risk prediction and return-to-sport evaluation across athletic populations, concluding that force plate metrics can reveal kinetic imbalances that precede both ACL injury and re-injury — but that predictive value depends on using multiple metrics within a multi-modal framework rather than relying on composite scores alone.

Research from OrthoSport Victoria's research unit, in collaboration with La Trobe University's Department of Allied Health — published in the Journal of ISAKOS in August 2025 — validated the Sparta Science force plate system specifically in ACL reconstruction patients at nine months post-operatively, finding moderate to excellent test-retest reliability (ICC > 0.96) and correlation with traditional return-to-sport testing measures including single and triple hop for distance.

A preprint published on medRxiv in September 2025 tracked a female varsity basketball player through ACL reconstruction using a three-domain framework — Capacity (force plate CMJ metrics), Capability (on-court inertial sensor loading), and Confidence (psychological readiness questionnaires) — and found that clearance in one domain did not predict clearance in another. The player's psychological readiness scores reached ceiling while her CMJ symmetry and on-court loading metrics were still realigning — illustrating that force plate data and self-reported readiness can point in different directions and that both need to be tracked independently.

Taken together, the 2025 research makes the same argument from multiple directions: ACL return-to-play clearance in basketball requires objective, force-plate-informed data on movement asymmetry. Time and self-report are not enough. A battery of jump tests, tracked with standardised force plate metrics across the rehabilitation timeline, is the current evidence standard.
