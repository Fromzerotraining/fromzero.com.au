What Is Basketball Combine Testing?

The NBA Draft Combine is an annual multi-day event held in Chicago each May before the June draft. It is the sport's most standardised athletic testing event, where prospects are measured, medically assessed, interviewed, and put through a battery of physical tests in front of NBA coaches, general managers, and scouts. Since 2024, participation has been mandatory for any player wishing to be eligible for the draft.

The tests are not designed to measure basketball skill. They measure raw physical tools — the underlying athletic capacities that coaches and scouts use to project long-term potential and durability. The same tests are widely used by college programs and development coaches to assess players at every level, making the NBA Combine the sport's de facto physical testing benchmark.

The Six Key Tests and What They Measure

Standing Vertical Jump. Players jump straight up from a standstill, with no approach or arm swing loaded. This measures pure concentric leg power — how explosively the lower body can generate upward force from a static position. It is the most directly sport-specific of all combine tests, mirroring the mechanics of the shot-block, rebound tap, and post-entry jump.

Maximum Vertical Jump. Players get a running approach before jumping. This version captures the full stretch-shortening cycle — the body's ability to store elastic energy in the eccentric (landing) phase and release it explosively in the concentric (takeoff) phase. It produces significantly higher numbers than the standing vertical and is the figure most commonly cited in player evaluations.

Lane Agility Drill. Players start in the centre of the key and sprint side-to-side before returning to the middle. It measures lateral quickness and reaction time — the capacity to change direction rapidly, which is foundational for on-ball defence, defensive rotations, and guard play in transition.

Three-Quarter Court Sprint. Players sprint the length of three-quarters of the court. This tests linear speed and acceleration — how fast a player covers ground in open court. It is particularly relevant for transition play, defensive close-outs, and the first-step quickness that separates elite defenders.

Shuttle Run. A multi-directional speed and agility drill measuring change-of-direction capacity under fatigue. The shuttle is closely related to the lane agility drill but emphasises a different directional plane.

Bench Press (185 lb / 83.9 kg for max reps). Players perform as many repetitions as possible at a fixed weight. This tests upper body strength endurance — the capacity to hold position against contact, finish through defenders, and sustain physical effort across a full game. It is the one upper-body test in the combine battery.

Physical measurements taken alongside the tests include height (with and without shoes), wingspan, standing reach, weight, body fat percentage, and hand length and width. These anthropometrics are combined with test results to build a complete athletic profile.

The 2025 Research Finding: Jump Height Predicts Injury Risk

A peer-reviewed study published in the Journal of Experimental Orthopaedics on 7 July 2025 is the first research to directly link NBA Combine scores to future lower limb surgical injury. The study, led by Ravi A. Patel and colleagues, analysed 27,105 injury transactions across 10 consecutive NBA seasons (2010–2020) and identified 130 players who underwent lower limb surgery during that period.

Researchers matched those players' pre-draft NBA Combine data to a non-injured control group and ran statistical comparisons. The finding that emerged was specific and significant: players who subsequently required lower limb surgery had measurably higher vertical jump scores at the Combine than players who did not.

What the Data Actually Shows

The statistical differences were as follows. Mean standing vertical jump was 73.86 cm in the non-injured group versus 76.00 cm in the group that later required surgery. Mean maximum vertical jump was 86.89 cm in the non-injured group versus 89.31 cm in the surgically managed group. Both differences were statistically significant at p < 0.05.

By contrast, lane agility time, three-quarter court sprint, and maximum bench press showed no statistically significant differences between the injured and non-injured groups. Anthropometric variables including weight, body fat percentage, and height also showed no significant differences. The injury signal was specific to vertical jump — not to size, speed, or strength as measured by the other combine tests.

Of the 130 surgically managed lower limb injuries identified, knee injuries accounted for 80.0% of cases. The breakdown of knee surgery types was: general knee surgery (42.3%), meniscal surgery (20.2%), arthroscopic knee surgery (18.3%), ACL reconstruction (15.4%), and patellar tendon repair (3.8%). Ankle (8.5%), calf (7.7%), and thigh (3.8%) injuries made up the remainder.

The authors note that this is a retrospective, correlational study. It establishes that a relationship exists between high vertical jump scores and later surgical intervention — it does not establish that jumping high causes injury. The mechanism behind the association requires further research. One hypothesis the authors raise is that players with high vertical jump ability may place greater eccentric load on the knee and lower limb structures over a career, increasing cumulative structural stress. However, this is not confirmed by the current data.

Why This Matters for Developing Players

This is the first large-scale study to examine NBA Combine data through an injury-prediction lens. Its implications extend well beyond the draft room. For strength and conditioning coaches working with developing basketball players, the finding introduces a meaningful question: are the most explosively gifted athletes also the ones most at risk, and should their training and monitoring be structured accordingly?

The practical takeaway is not to avoid developing vertical jump. Jump training is foundational to basketball performance and is supported by extensive evidence as the most effective training modality for improving the physical capacities the game demands. The takeaway is that high jumpers may warrant additional injury screening, movement quality assessment, and load management — particularly in high-volume training environments where the cumulative stress on the knee can accumulate without detection.

The study also highlights that the NBA Combine tests, despite being the most widely used athletic benchmarks in basketball, do not comprehensively capture injury risk. A player's combine profile tells you what their body can produce at a moment in time. It does not tell you how that capacity has been trained, how their mechanics handle landing forces, or how their joint health has been managed. All of that context lives outside the combine numbers.

For coaches and programs building athlete profiles, the recommendation is to treat combine-style testing as one layer of a broader assessment — not as a standalone picture of athletic readiness or durability.

2025 NBA Combine Standouts

The 2025 NBA Draft Combine was held 11–18 May at Wintrust Arena and the Marriott Marquis in Chicago, with 75 invited participants. From 2024, participation became mandatory for draft eligibility. The 2025 testing produced several notable athletic performances.

North Carolina wing Drake Powell led the entire Combine in both standing and maximum vertical jump, recording a 37.5-inch standing vertical and a 43.0-inch maximum vertical. He also measured 6'5.25" barefoot with a 7'0" wingspan — a combination of length and explosiveness that moved his draft stock significantly. Projected No. 1 overall pick Cooper Flagg posted a 35.5-inch maximum vertical while also excelling in the three-quarter court sprint and lane agility drill, reinforcing his status as the class's most complete prospect.

The 2025 Combine also saw Penn State big man Yanic Konan Niederhauser post the highest raw athleticism score in the class for his position group, recording an elite shuttle run time and a 37-inch maximum vertical at 242.6 pounds — exceptional for a player his size.

Combine Benchmarks Worth Knowing

For players using NBA Combine tests to benchmark their own development, context matters. The following are representative ranges from the official NBA Combine database for players who have been invited to the event over recent years. These figures reflect players who have already been identified as draft-level prospects and represent a high competitive baseline.

Maximum vertical jump at the Combine typically ranges from the low-to-mid 30 inches for larger, less explosive players up to the low-to-mid 40-inch range for elite leapers. The all-time Combine record is 46 inches, set by D.J. Stephens in 2013. A player recording a 38–40 inch maximum vertical is in the top tier of the tested population. Three-quarter court sprint times for elite prospects typically fall in the 3.0–3.2 second range, with guards generally testing faster than bigs. Lane agility times under 11 seconds are considered strong. Bench press repetitions at 185 lb vary enormously by position and training background and are less predictive of draft position than other tests.

Wingspan differential — the difference between wingspan and standing height — is one of the most scrutinised anthropometric variables. A positive wingspan differential (wingspan greater than height) is consistently valued in evaluations at all levels. It extends defensive reach and rebounding radius without adding bulk, and it cannot be trained.
